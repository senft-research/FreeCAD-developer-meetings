## Policy on External Build Utilities in the Main Repo

The discussion began with an overview of the current situation regarding FreeCAD build utilities. While Pixi is the officially supported method and is well-maintained within the main repository, many users employ alternative methods such as distribution-specific builds (e.g., Ubuntu), LibPack, and Nix. Additionally, external build systems like Homebrew for macOS and Snap are used, and some internal methods like RPM builders exist. The presence of outdated Docker files within the main repository was also noted. The core question was whether to keep all build-related files within the main repository or to encourage external maintenance, especially for builds not requiring testing against the master branch. The consensus leaned towards having only Pixi in the main repository, with other build utilities in separate repositories to avoid confusion and reduce maintenance overhead. The idea of a single "mono repo" for all build utilities was largely dismissed. 

An action item was assigned to Chris Hennes to create these separate repositories and identify maintainers for them.

## AI-Generated Patches

Louis raises concerns about the increasing number of LLM-generated patches, noting their use in descriptions, commit messages, and discussions. He finds this concerning due to the strain on maintainers, the low quality of many contributions, and the time required for review. He questions if others share this concern or if he is being paranoid, suggesting a review of the contribution policy. Turan confirms that this topic has been discussed extensively over the past six months and the current policy is the best that could be achieved given the range of opinions among contributors.

Jackson suggests integrating AI review tools like CodeRabbit to filter out low-quality submissions before they reach human reviewers. John expresses strong opposition to AI in open source, citing the use of stolen open-source code, and observes a similar problem in the add-on ecosystem with low-quality, unmaintained AI-generated add-ons. Jordan shares the sentiment, highlighting the risk of humans becoming lazy and losing skills due to over-reliance on LLMs, even in their own work. Rehan believes AI is useful for commit messages and documentation proofreading but cautions against its inaccuracies, citing a personal experience with incorrect Qt version information. Benjamin views AI as a tool, emphasizing the need for human oversight and ensuring contributions are maintainable. He also mentions the NLNet Foundation's relevant policy on generative AI.

Giuseppe asks about practical methods to combat AI-generated PRs and regain time lost to reviewing low-quality code. Chris Hennes clarifies that Jackson's earlier mention of CodeRabbit is an AI review tool. Benjamin reiterates that the capacity of AI is rapidly improving, making it difficult to distinguish human from AI contributions. He suggests maintainers should carefully assess the maintainability of PRs, regardless of their origin.

Louis circles back to the original topic, asking if PRs clearly written by AI should be rejected outright, citing Godot's policy. He questions the current practice of discussing with contributors who may be using chatbots themselves. Benjamin states that maintainers can close LLM-generated PRs if they deem it necessary, emphasizing that code quality is paramount, whether human or AI-generated. He notes that it's up to the maintainer's discretion to invest time in reviewing such contributions.

Benjamin argued that code, regardless of its source, must be well-written and that sloppy code, whether from an LLM or a human, should not be accepted. He acknowledged the difficulty in saying no to contributors and the tendency to give the benefit of the doubt, recalling a personal experience where he mistook an LLM for a human. Benjamin suggested that while direct assumption is difficult, maintainers can flag contributions that appear to be from an LLM and place them in draft status for review.

Jackson agreed with Benjamin's point about not turning away contributors, especially if the focus is on human contributions over machine contributions. He expressed his personal opinion that the quality of the code and the contribution should be the sole determining factors, irrespective of whether a machine assisted in its creation. Jackson concluded by stating that the origin of the code is orthogonal to its quality.

John clarified the purpose of an "no AI" policy, stating that it would represent the "official stance" and desired practice, similar to laws. He acknowledged that laws can be broken, but the point is that the activity is officially not allowed or wanted. John agreed that determining AI-generated content can be difficult, but most commits are obvious. He reiterated that the core of the policy would be to take a stance against such contributions and to reserve the right to reject them.

Jordan raised two points. First, he reminded users that the subsidization phase for AI tools is ending, citing OpenAI's API as an example where the cost per million tokens has significantly increased from a dollar or two to $180. He advised those on the "pro AI" side to consider potential future costs based on policy decisions. Second, Jordan suggested that a good mitigation strategy, without necessarily changing current policies, would be to hold regular discussions on AI at developer meetings, perhaps every other month. He proposed a brief check-up of opinions to ensure things are progressing well, emphasizing that this would be a reasonable middle ground.
